## 1. How Lawn Bowling Works

### The Objective

The objective of lawn bowls is simple: roll your bowls so they come to rest as close as possible to a small target ball called the **jack** (also called the "kitty" or "mark"). The team or player with one or more bowls closer to the jack than any of their opponent's bowls scores points ("shots") for that **end**. A game consists of multiple ends.

### The Green

- A **bowling green** is a flat, manicured grass (or synthetic) surface, square or rectangular.
- Dimensions: **31 to 40 metres** in the direction of play, and up to 60 metres wide.
- The green is divided into parallel lanes called **rinks**. Each rink is **4.3 to 5.8 metres wide**.
- A typical green has **6 rinks**, allowing 6 games to be played simultaneously, though this varies by green size.
- The green is surrounded by a shallow trench called the **ditch** (about 200-380mm wide, 50-200mm deep).
- Beyond the ditch is the **bank** -- a raised border.
- Rinks are marked with **boundary pegs** on the bank and a **centre line** running down the middle.

### The Jack

- A small, solid, spherical ball -- **63 to 67mm in diameter**.
- Coloured **white or yellow**.
- The jack is **unbiased** (rolls in a straight line, unlike the bowls).
- The jack is the target. At the start of each end, the lead rolls it down the green to set the target distance.
- The jack must travel at least **23 metres** from the mat to be "in play."
- Once at rest, the jack is centred on the rink (moved laterally to the centre line).

### The Bowls

- Larger balls, **112mm to 134mm in diameter**, weighing up to **1.59 kg**.
- Made of composite resin (historically lignum vitae wood -- hence the term "woods").
- There are **9 standard sizes** numbered 0000 through 5. Players choose a size that fits their hand.
- Each player uses a **matched set of bowls** (2 or 4 depending on format) with identical markings.
- Bowls have small **circular rings** (called "discs") on each side for identification. The **smaller ring indicates the bias side**.

### Bias -- The Defining Feature

Bias is what makes lawn bowls unique. Every bowl is **asymmetrical** -- one side is slightly flatter/heavier than the other. This causes the bowl to travel in a **curved arc** rather than a straight line.

**How bias works:**
- When rolling fast, the bowl travels relatively straight.
- As it **slows down**, the bias takes increasing effect, curving the bowl toward the heavier (bias) side.
- The curve becomes most pronounced in the final few metres before the bowl stops.
- The **smaller concentric ring** on the bowl marks the bias side -- the side toward which it will curve.
- Different bowl models have different amounts of bias. Narrow-bias bowls curve less (better for leads on fast greens). Wide-bias bowls curve more (preferred by skips for drawing around obstacles).

**Playing with bias:**
- Players must aim **wide of their target** (called "taking grass") and let the bias bring the bowl back toward the jack.
- A **forehand** delivery has the bowl curving from the dominant-hand side inward.
- A **backhand** delivery has the bowl curving from the non-dominant side.
- Choosing the right hand (forehand vs backhand) is a tactical decision based on what obstacles are in the way.

### The Mat

- A rectangular mat (360mm x 600mm minimum for outdoor play) placed on the rink to mark the delivery point.
- The player delivering must have at least one foot on or over the mat at the moment of release (**foot fault** if violated).
- The lead places the mat at the start of each end. Its position can be varied tactically -- placing it further up the green shortens the playing distance.

### Delivery (How You Bowl)

1. Stand on the mat, feet together or staggered.
2. Hold the bowl with the bias oriented correctly (small ring toward the direction you want it to curve).
3. Step forward, swing the arm smoothly, and release the bowl at ground level.
4. The bowl should land smoothly on the green without bouncing ("dumping" is poor technique).
5. Follow through with the arm pointing in the direction of the aiming line.

### Sequence of Play in an End

1. **Mat placement:** The team that won the previous end (or won the coin toss for the first end) places the mat.
2. **Jack delivery:** The lead from the mat-placing team rolls the jack down the green. It must travel at least 23m. If it goes in the ditch or off the rink, the opposing lead delivers it.
3. **Jack centring:** Once the jack stops, it is moved laterally to the centre line of the rink.
4. **Bowling:** Players from each team alternate delivering their bowls (Lead A bowls one, Lead B bowls one, Lead A bowls their second, Lead B their second, then Seconds, etc.).
5. **Determining the count:** When all bowls have been delivered, the team with the bowl closest to the jack scores. They score one point ("shot") for each of their bowls that is closer to the jack than the opponent's nearest bowl.
6. **Next end:** Play reverses direction. The team that scored places the mat and delivers the jack from the opposite end.

### The Head

The **head** is the cluster of bowls and the jack at the far end of the rink. Reading the head -- understanding which bowls are closest, what gaps exist, where to place your next bowl -- is the core tactical skill of the game.

## 2. Player Positions and Roles

In team formats (Pairs, Triples, Fours), each player has a designated position with specific responsibilities. Positions bowl in order: Lead first, then Second (in Fours), then Third/Vice-Skip, then Skip last.

### Lead

**Bowls:** First in order of play
**Primary focus:** Foundation and consistency

| Responsibility | Detail |
|---|---|
| Place the mat | Positions it per the skip's instruction |
| Deliver the jack | Must roll it to the agreed length consistently |
| Draw to the jack | Primary shot is the draw -- getting close to the jack |
| Set up the head | Establish a favourable position early |
| Provide information | Their bowls show teammates the line and weight needed |

**Shot types:** Almost exclusively **draw shots**. Leads rarely play aggressive or weighted shots. Consistency and accuracy are valued above all else.

**At the head end:** When not bowling, the lead stands behind the head and stays out of the way. In Pairs, the lead measures shots and agrees the count with the opposing lead.

### Second

**Bowls:** Second in order (exists only in Fours format)
**Primary focus:** Strengthening and applying pressure

| Responsibility | Detail |
|---|---|
| Reinforce the head | Build on the lead's position |
| Add cover | Place bowls behind or beside the jack as insurance |
| Adapt | Respond to what the head looks like after the leads have bowled |
| Keep the scorecard | In Fours, the second traditionally keeps score |

**Shot types:** Primarily draw shots, but begins to develop **controlled variations** -- positional bowls, up-shots (slightly weighted to push bowls), and cover shots. The second is where versatility starts to matter.

**Tactical role:** "Apply steady pressure that forces the opposition to take risks later." The second's job is to make the skip's life easier.

### Third (Vice-Skip)

**Bowls:** Third in order (Triples and Fours)
**Primary focus:** Information, tactics, and decision support

| Responsibility | Detail |
|---|---|
| Direct play at the head | When the skip is at the mat end bowling, the third takes charge at the head end, directing play |
| Communicate head status | Tells the skip how many shots up or down, where bowls are, what shot to play |
| Measure disputed shots | Uses calipers/tape to determine which bowl is closer |
| Agree the count | With the opposing third, determines and agrees how many shots were scored before moving any bowls |
| Support the skip's decisions | Provides tactical input but defers to the skip's authority |

**Shot types:** Variable -- must be able to play whatever the head demands. Draw, trail (moving the jack), wick (deflecting off another bowl), or weighted shots.

**Tactical role:** Described as "the tactical engine of the team." The third must read the head accurately and communicate clearly. Poor communication from a third costs games.

### Skip

**Bowls:** Last in order (always)
**Primary focus:** Leadership, strategy, and execution under pressure

| Responsibility | Detail |
|---|---|
| Direct all play | Stands at the head end and signals/instructs each player what shot to play |
| Set game strategy | Decides jack length, mat position, overall tactical approach |
| Bowl last | Delivers the final (and often decisive) bowls of each end |
| Manage the team | Handles morale, momentum, and personnel |
| Make the big calls | Decides when to play safe vs aggressive, when to change tactics |

**Shot types:** Must master **all shots** -- draw, drive (heavy weight to blast bowls out), trail, wick, block, promotion (nudging own bowl closer). The skip often needs to rescue bad ends or capitalize on good ones.

**At the mat end:** When it is the skip's turn to bowl, they walk to the mat end and the third takes over directing play from the head end.

**Tactical role:** "Sees the bigger picture -- not just the head, but the score, conditions, momentum, and emotional state of the team." The skip is the captain.

### Position Summary Table

| Position | Order | Bowls (Fours) | Bowls (Triples) | Bowls (Pairs) | Primary Skill |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lead | 1st | 2 | 3 | 4 | Draw accuracy |
| Second | 2nd | 2 | -- | -- | Versatility |
| Third/Vice | 3rd | 2 | 3 | -- | Tactical reading |
| Skip | 4th (last) | 2 | 3 | 4 | All-round, leadership |

**Note:** The user mentioned "Triples = 3 teams of 2" and "Pairs = 4 teams of 2." This does NOT match standard lawn bowls terminology. In standard bowls, every game is **always 2 sides per rink**. The user may be describing a **social/club draw format** where, for example, 6 people are drawn onto one rink and split into 3 pairs playing a round-robin on that rink, or 8 people split into 4 pairs. This is a **social bowls variation**, not a standard competition format. The app should handle both standard formats and these social draw variations.

### Singles

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Players per side | 1 |
| Bowls per player | 4 |
| Total bowls per end | 8 (4 each) |
| Standard ends | First to **21 shots** (not ends-based) |
| Alternative format | Sets play: 2 sets of 9 ends, tiebreaker of 3 ends |
| Marker | A non-playing **marker** assists by centring the jack, marking touchers, and answering distance questions |

### Pairs

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Players per side | 2 (Lead + Skip) |
| Bowls per player | 4 |
| Total bowls per end | 16 (8 per team) |
| Standard ends | **21 ends** (championship) |
| Shorter games | Often 15 or 18 ends for social/club play |
| Scoring | Total shots across all ends; highest total wins |

**Order of play:** Lead A, Lead B, Lead A, Lead B (each delivers all 4), then Skip A, Skip B, Skip A, Skip B.

### Triples

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Players per side | 3 (Lead + Second + Skip) |
| Bowls per player | 3 |
| Total bowls per end | 18 (9 per team) |
| Standard ends | **18 ends** (championship) |
| Shorter games | Often 15 ends for social play |
| Scoring | Total shots across all ends; highest total wins |
| Variation | 2-bowl triples (faster game, 6 bowls per team per end) |

**Note on roles:** In triples there is no dedicated "Third/Vice-Skip" position. The second player performs some vice-skip duties (measuring, communicating). The Lead delivers the jack and draws. The Second reinforces. The Skip directs and bowls last.

### Fours (Rinks)

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Players per side | 4 (Lead + Second + Third + Skip) |
| Bowls per player | 2 |
| Total bowls per end | 16 (8 per team) |
| Standard ends | **21 ends** (championship) |
| Shorter games | Often 15 or 18 ends for social/club play |
| Scoring | Total shots across all ends; highest total wins |

This is the **traditional team format** and the basis of **pennant (league) competition**. Each player has a clearly defined role. With only 2 bowls each, every delivery matters -- there is less margin for error than in pairs or triples.

**Order of play:** Lead A, Lead B, Second A, Second B, Third A, Third B, Skip A, Skip B -- each delivering both their bowls before the next pair begins.

### Social Bowls Variations

These are the formats most relevant to the Pick a Partner app:

**Tabs-In / Names-in-the-Hat:**
- Players arrive at the club and put their name in (literally a tab/ticket in a container, or sign a sheet).
- At the designated start time, teams are **drawn randomly**.
- The organizer determines the format (pairs, triples, or fours) based on how many players showed up.
- Teams are assigned to rinks, also randomly.

**Multi-Game Social Format:**
- Common format: **3 or 4 games of 7-8 ends** each.
- After each game, teams are **re-drawn** so everyone plays with and against different people.
- Individual scores are tallied across all games. The player with the highest cumulative score wins a prize.
- This is a "getting to know you" format -- ideal for clubs wanting to mix members.

**Pick-a-Partner:**
- Players choose their own partner (rather than random draw).
- Typically played as pairs.
- May be organized as a one-day tournament or a regular club event.
- This is the **namesake format of the app**.

**Two-Bowl Formats:**
- Many social formats use **2 bowls per player** regardless of team size to speed up play.
- A 2-bowl triples game averages about 40 minutes.
- Reduces the time commitment and keeps the pace lively.

## 4. Tournament Structures

### Round-Robin

- Every team plays every other team once (single round-robin) or twice (double round-robin).
- **Fairest format** -- no team is eliminated early, every matchup occurs.
- **Downside:** Time-consuming with many teams. For N teams, requires N(N-1)/2 games.
- Common in club championships and smaller tournaments.
- Results tracked by: wins, then shots differential, then total shots scored.

### Knockout (Elimination)

- **Single elimination:** Lose once and you are out. Fast, dramatic, but a single bad game ends your tournament.
- **Double elimination:** Must lose twice to be eliminated. Fairer but more complex.
- Common in larger tournaments and championship events.
- Seeding may be used to keep top teams apart in early rounds.

### Swiss System

- Players/teams with similar records play each other ("winners play winners, losers play losers").
- **All teams play the same number of rounds** -- nobody is eliminated.
- Fewer rounds needed than round-robin (typically log2(N) rounds for N teams).
- Good for large fields where round-robin is impractical.
- Used in some progressive bowls tournaments.

### Sectional Play

- Teams divided into **sections** (groups).
- Round-robin within each section.
- Section winners advance to knockout rounds or a finals section.
- Common structure for **pennant competitions** (leagues).

### Pennant (League) Competition

This is the primary competitive format at Australian bowls clubs:

- Run by the regional/state bowls association (e.g., Bowls Victoria, Bowls WA).
- Clubs enter one or more **sides** (teams of 12-16 players, divided into rinks of 4).
- Played weekly across a **season** (typically October-March in Australia, April-September in UK).
- Multiple **divisions** based on club/player strength (Division 1 through 6+).
- Each match: 3 rinks of fours, 21 ends per rink (63 ends total per match).
- Points awarded for: rink wins, overall shots, match result.
- **Selection committees** at each club choose the team each week.
- Promotion/relegation between divisions based on season results.

### Social Tournament Formats

Most relevant to the app:

**Progressive Pairs/Triples:**
- Multiple short games (7-8 ends).
- After each game, winners move up one rink, losers stay (or vice versa).
- Partners may rotate between games.
- Individual scores accumulated over all games.

**Spider:**
- A novelty event: all players place their bowls at the edge of the green, then simultaneously bowl toward a jack in the centre.
- Closest bowl wins a prize.
- Used as a social icebreaker, not a competitive format.

**Drawn Pairs/Triples Tournament:**
- Entry is individual. Partners are drawn randomly.
- Round-robin or knockout over a day.
- Prizes for winning team, sometimes individual prizes.

### Draw Mechanics

How teams are assigned to rinks for a round of play:

1. Each team is assigned a **random number**.
2. Teams sorted by random number.
3. Teams allocated to **rinks** in order (Rink 1: Team 1 vs Team 2, Rink 2: Team 3 vs Team 4, etc.).
4. For multi-round events, constraints applied:
   - No team plays on the same rink twice (if possible).
   - No two teams play each other more than once (if possible).
   - Balance home/away (mat placement advantage).

## 5. Club Structure

A typical lawn bowls club (especially in Australia, UK, NZ):

| Attribute | Typical Value |
|---|---|

| Category | Description | Bowling Rights | Voting Rights |

**Executive Committee (Board):**
- **President** -- overall leadership, chairs meetings
- **Vice President** -- deputises for president
- **Secretary** -- correspondence, minutes, administration
- **Treasurer** -- finances, budgets, accounts
- **Club Captain** -- manages all bowling activities (selection, match days)
- **Committee Members** (3-5) -- elected, portfolio responsibilities

**Sub-Committees:**

| Committee | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **Bowls/Match Committee** | Competition scheduling, pennant selection, tournament organization, match-day operations |

| **Bar Committee** | Bar operations, stock, licensing compliance |
| **Membership Committee** | Recruitment, new member induction, retention |
| **Finance Committee** | Budget oversight, fee setting, grant applications |

### Selection Committee

Critical for the app context:
- Typically 3-5 members (Club Captain + selectors).
- Responsible for choosing pennant teams each week.
- Must consider: player availability, form, positions, compatibility.
- Historically done via phone calls and paper lists -- a major pain point.
- Players indicate **availability** for each match day.
- The selector then forms rinks (teams of 4) and assigns positions.

### Typical Club Calendar

| Time | Activity |
|---|---|
| Early morning (7-9am) | Green maintenance, roll-ups |
| Morning (9-11am) | Social bowls, coaching |
| Afternoon (1-4pm) | Pennant matches (Sat), social bowls (other days) |

### Social Aspects

Lawn bowls clubs are as much **social institutions** as sporting ones:
- Many members join primarily for social connection.
- Clubs host functions: trivia nights, dinners, barefoot bowls evenings.
- The bar is a significant revenue source and social hub.
- "Rolling up" (casual practice) is a social activity.
- Post-game drinks and discussion are a tradition.
- Volunteering (green maintenance, bar duty, event organization) is expected.

## 6. Scoring Systems

### Basic Scoring: Shots per End

After all bowls are delivered in an end:

1. Identify which team has the **closest bowl** to the jack.
2. That team scores **one shot for each of their bowls** that is closer to the jack than the opposing team's nearest bowl.
3. The opposing team scores **zero** for that end.
4. If the closest bowls from each team are **equidistant** from the jack, the end is a **tied end** (no score, or "no score end").

**Example:** Team A has bowls at 30cm, 45cm, and 90cm from the jack. Team B's nearest bowl is at 60cm. Team A scores **2 shots** (the 30cm and 45cm bowls are both closer than Team B's 60cm bowl). The 90cm bowl does not count.

### Scorecard

| End | Team A | Team B |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | 2 | -- |
| 2 | -- | 3 |
| 3 | 1 | -- |
| 4 | -- | 1 |
| Running total | 3 | 4 |

### Winning Conditions by Format

| Format | How Winner is Determined |
|---|---|
| **Singles** | First player to **21 shots** (or sets play: best of 2 sets of 9 ends, with 3-end tiebreaker) |
| **Pairs** | Most total shots after **21 ends** |
| **Triples** | Most total shots after **18 ends** |
| **Fours** | Most total shots after **21 ends** |
| **Social games** | Most total shots after agreed number of ends (often 10-15) |

### Sets Play (Alternative Scoring)

Used in some singles and modern format competitions:

- Match divided into **sets** (typically 2 sets of 9 ends).
- The player with the most shots at the end of a set **wins the set**.
- Win 2 sets = win the match.
- If each player wins one set, a **3-end tiebreaker** is played.
- If a set is tied on shots, the set is **halved** (shared).

### Tie-Breaking Methods

When teams are tied at the end of regulation play:

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Extra end** | Play one (or more) additional ends. First team ahead after an extra end wins. |
| **Shots differential** | In multi-game tournaments: total shots scored minus total shots conceded. |
| **Total shots scored** | If differential is equal, highest total shots for. |
| **Head-to-head** | Result of the game between the tied teams. |
| **Coin toss** | Last resort (rare). |

### Pennant Match Scoring

In pennant (league) competition, each match involves multiple rinks:

- **Rink points:** Each rink result earns points (e.g., 2 points for a rink win, 1 for a draw).
- **Match result:** The team winning the majority of rinks (or having the highest aggregate shots) may earn bonus points.
- **Season ladder:** Teams ranked by total points, then shots differential.

### Score Terminology

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Shot** | One point (also: the closest bowl is "the shot bowl") |
| **Count/Counting** | Bowls that are scoring (e.g., "we have 3 counting") |
| **Up** | Your team is closest ("we are holding" or "we are up") |
| **Down** | Opponent is closest ("we are down 2") |
| **Full count** | All your team's bowls are closer than any of the opponent's |
| **No score** | Tied end, equidistant nearest bowls |

```
World Bowls
  |

    |
    Bowls Victoria / Bowls NSW / Bowls WA / Bowls Qld / etc.
      |
      Regional/District Associations (e.g., Geelong Bowls Region)
        |

```

Each level handles:
- **World Bowls:** Laws of the sport, world championships, international standards
- **National body:** National championships, national team selection, strategic direction
- **State body:** Pennant competitions, state championships, club affiliation, umpire accreditation
- **District/Region:** Local pennant administration, inter-club events, district championships
- **Club:** Day-to-day operations, social bowls, member management, internal competitions

### Commonwealth Games

Lawn bowls has been included in the **Commonwealth Games** since 1930 (men's) and 1986 (women's). It is one of the sport's highest-profile events and a major driver of participation in Commonwealth countries.

## 8. Key Terminology / Glossary

This glossary focuses on terms the Pick a Partner app will likely need to understand and use.

### Game Structure

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **End** | One complete sequence of play: jack delivered, all bowls delivered, score counted. A game consists of multiple ends. |
| **Head** | The area around the jack where bowls have come to rest. "Reading the head" means assessing the positions of all bowls relative to the jack. |
| **Rink** | (1) The playing lane on a green (4.3-5.8m wide). (2) A team of four players (synonym for "Fours"). |
| **Green** | The entire playing surface, typically divided into 6 rinks. |
| **Mat** | The rubber mat from which players deliver their bowls. |
| **Ditch** | The trench around the edge of the green. A bowl in the ditch is dead unless it is a toucher. |
| **Bank** | The raised border beyond the ditch. |
| **Centre line** | The line running down the middle of each rink. |

### Equipment

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Bowl** | The biased ball players roll toward the jack. Also called "wood" (historically). |
| **Jack** | The small target ball (also "kitty" or "mark"). White or yellow, 63-67mm diameter. |
| **Bias** | The asymmetric weight distribution causing the bowl to curve. |
| **Disc/Ring** | Circular markings on bowls. Small ring = bias side. Large ring = non-bias side. |
| **Chalk** | Used to mark a toucher (bowl that touched the jack during delivery). |
| **Measure/Tape** | Used to determine which bowl is closest to the jack. |
| **Calipers** | Precision measuring device for very close bowls. |

### Delivery and Shots

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Draw** | The most fundamental shot: rolling a bowl to come to rest near the jack without disturbing other bowls. |
| **Drive** | A fast, forceful delivery intended to blast bowls out of the head or knock the jack into the ditch. |
| **Forehand** | Delivery where the bowl curves from the dominant-hand side inward. |
| **Backhand** | Delivery where the bowl curves from the non-dominant side. |
| **Weight** | The force/pace of a delivery. "Good weight" = correct speed to reach the target. |
| **Line** | The direction/aiming point of a delivery, accounting for bias. |
| **Grass** | The amount of width given to allow for bias. "More grass" = aim wider. |
| **Yard on** | A delivery intended to finish about one yard past the jack. |
| **Trail** | A shot that moves the jack to a new position (usually backward toward your back bowls). |
| **Wick** | When a bowl deflects off another bowl and changes direction. |
| **Promote** | Pushing one of your own team's bowls closer to the jack with a weighted shot. |
| **Block/Blocker** | A bowl placed short of the head to obstruct the opponent's line of delivery. |
| **Cover** | A bowl placed behind or to the side of the jack as insurance in case the jack is moved. |
| **Kill** | A deliberate attempt to send the jack into the ditch (dead end) or off the rink. |

### Positions and Results

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Shot bowl** | The bowl currently closest to the jack. |
| **Holding** | Your team currently has the shot bowl ("we're holding"). |
| **Up/Down** | "Up 2" = your team has 2 bowls counting. "Down 1" = opponent has 1 counting. |
| **Counting/Counters** | Bowls that are currently scoring (closer than opponent's nearest). |
| **Toucher** | A bowl that touched the jack during its delivery. Marked with chalk. Remains live even if it goes in the ditch. |
| **Dead bowl** | A bowl that has gone in the ditch (without being a toucher), off the rink, or is otherwise out of play. |
| **Dead end/Burnt end** | An end that must be replayed because the jack went out of bounds. |
| **Jack high** | A bowl that has come to rest level with the jack (same distance from the mat, but to the side). |
| **Short** | A bowl that stops before reaching the jack. |
| **Heavy** | A bowl delivered with too much weight, going past the target. |
| **Narrow** | A bowl that did not take enough grass -- it curves too far toward the centre. |
| **Wide** | A bowl that took too much grass -- it finishes too far from centre. |
| **Wrong bias** | A bowl delivered with the bias facing the wrong way, causing it to curve away from the target. An embarrassing error. |

### Competition and Club

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Pennant** | The main inter-club league competition, run by the state/regional association. |
| **Division** | Skill-based tiers within pennant competition (Division 1 = strongest). |
| **Section** | A group of teams within a division who play each other in round-robin. |
| **Selection/Selectors** | The process/people who choose which players play in pennant teams and in which positions. |
| **Roll-up** | An informal practice session (also "roll up"). |
| **Tabs-in** | A system where players put their name in for social bowls; teams are then drawn randomly. |
| **Barefoot bowls** | Casual lawn bowls played without bowling shoes, often with food/drinks. Popular for corporate events and attracting younger players. |
| **Spider** | A novelty event where all players bowl simultaneously from the edges toward a central jack. |
| **Marker** | A non-playing official who assists in singles games (centres the jack, marks touchers, measures). |
| **Umpire** | Official who adjudicates disputes, measures shots, enforces rules. |
| **Walkover** | A win awarded when the opposing team fails to show up. |
| **Trial end / Practice end** | Non-scoring ends at the start of a match for warm-up. |

### Social/Organizational

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Draw** | (1) A draw shot. (2) The process of randomly assigning teams/rinks. (3) The published schedule showing who plays whom. |
| **Fixture** | A scheduled match or set of matches. |
| **Availability** | A player's indication of whether they can play on a given day. |
| **Side** | A club's full team for a pennant match (e.g., 12-16 players across 3-4 rinks). |
| **Skip** | (1) The team captain/last bowler. (2) Verb: to captain a team ("who's skipping today?"). |

## App Design Implications

Based on this research, key concepts the Pick a Partner app must model:

### Core Data Entities
- **Players** -- with position preferences (lead/second/third/skip), skill level, availability
- **Teams** -- dynamically formed groups of 2-4 players with assigned positions
- **Rinks** -- physical playing lanes with numbers, belonging to a green
- **Greens** -- the playing surface containing multiple rinks
- **Games** -- a match between 2 teams on a rink, with a format (pairs/triples/fours) and number of ends
- **Ends** -- individual scoring rounds within a game
- **Tournaments/Events** -- a collection of games with a structure (round-robin, knockout, etc.)
- **Draws** -- the assignment of teams to rinks for a round of play

### Key Workflows
1. **Player check-in** -- "I'm here and available to play"
2. **Team formation** -- Random draw or partner selection based on format
3. **Rink assignment** -- Allocating teams to available rinks
4. **Score entry** -- End-by-end scoring during play
5. **Results tracking** -- Game results, tournament standings, individual statistics
6. **Availability management** -- Players indicating which days they can play

### Format-Specific Logic
- The app needs to handle the **variable number of bowls per player** by format (2 in fours, 3 in triples, 4 in pairs/singles).
- Scoring is always shots-based but the win condition varies (first to 21 in singles vs most shots after N ends in team formats).
- Social formats need flexible team generation from a pool of available players.
- The draw algorithm must handle constraints (no repeat matchups, distribute rinks fairly).
